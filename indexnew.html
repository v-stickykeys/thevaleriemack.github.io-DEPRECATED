Game Design

Clone Combat

Play on Newgrounds | Play on Kongregate

Clone Combat is a game that I created with classmates for our Analytics-Driven Game Design course. I came up with the concept for this puzzle platformer where the player has to defeat clones who mirror each of the player's inputs in real time. We successfully executed our ideas, creating three iterative versions that improved over each design cycle.

My primary roles were user interface and user experience designer.

After analyzing our data from play testers, I concluded that our greatest design challenge was learnability, so I created comprehensive tutorial levels that increased player engagement. (See slides on top right and bottom left.)

Clone Combat was one of the most successful games produced from our class. It was featured on Newgrounds and received a 3.5 / 5 rating and over 20,000 plays in the first week. With a tougher audience on Kongregate, we received a 3.1 / 5 star rating.

UX research & design

Augmate

I was fortunate enough to be a UX intern for the wearable tech startup Augmate, through the Grand Central Tech accelerator. One of my main projects was to design a research experiment to test usability of the scanning feature that the programming team developed.

We created a room booking application to use as a test prototype, and I recruited 5 early adopters who had little to no experience with Glass to do user testing. Participants were tasked with reserving a room for a meeting using our Google Glass application.

Through observation and structured qualitative interviews I identified patterns in users’ activities, attitudes, and aptitudes. While participants understood how to use the app to accomplish their task, they had difficulty operating our barcode scanner and got confused on what type of input was required for each action.

These findings were incorporated into the development of the Augmate warehouse application for our client UPS. The programming team focused on increasing the accessible range of the scanner and reducing scanning speed.

I began drafting multiple versions of potential interface designs to make instructions more clear. (See "Cycle Counting" image below.)

In addition to my design work, I had an active role in faciliating communication between members of the engineering, business, and design teams.

Mechanical Dreams

I did not have a formal internship this past summer, so I had to create one from scratch. Using my deveoping interest in wearables, I turned to the watchmaking industry to learn more about the history of mechanical timepieces and where they are headed as technological capabilites develop further.

After networking at a wearables roundtable hosted at Aaron Faber gallery, I became the apprentice to a Manhattan watchmaker with 30+ years of experience at Tourneau, Rolex, and Tiffany & Co.

Throughout the summer, I used the knowledge I was gaining from my apprenticeship to design an educational watchmaking game for Art of Horology, the only watchmaking school in Manhattan. I was responsible for overseeing the project, and designing the gameplay and the user interface.

Human Computer Interaction

Familiar Stranger Interaction

In my Advanced Human Computer Interaction course I explored familiar stranger interaction in dining places, which lead to an understanding of the barriers that prevent these interactions.

My team members and I used four research methods and designed two protoypes based on our hypothesis that there is a desire for the value that a more interactive local community provides.

Contrary to what much of the HCI community focuses on--we acknowledged a necessity to ignore capabilities that often lead to technology-based design solutions, in order to have a more thorough understanding of our problem, its effects on people, and how we can design for it. In doing so, we expanded the traditional idea of “design” to include undesign and non-design approaches.

UI design

gix science

Gix is a startup that came out of Cornell's ELab program. I was asked to be the designer for their web platform, aimed at transforming the way we do science.

Gix is a cloud-based data collection, analysis, and storage platform where users can share and display content using iPython notebooks. It is similar to github, as it allows researchers a form of version control that can be easily examined, replicated, and experimented with by others.

Gix also provides a unique way for researchers to be recognized for the work they have contributed to by keeping track of when and where their publications have been cited.
